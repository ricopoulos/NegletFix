Sprint 2 conclusion
Alex Bowers' material is a strong match for NegletFix's real-life layer: it supports measuring blind-side scanning, hazard/obstacle detection, reaction time, and navigation confidence. It should not be treated as a driving-clearance pathway.
Important boundary
Driving is a safety and licensing question. NegletFix can learn from driving-simulator research, but the app must never imply that a training score means Eric is safe or legal to drive. That belongs to clinicians, licensing rules, and specialized on-road or simulator evaluation.

3. Verified Claims

Claim Verification NegletFix Meaning
People with HH often miss hazards on the blind side, even in realistic simulator tasks. Driving With Hemianopia III and its PMC version report blind-side detection deficits for stationary and approaching pedestrians. Eric's app should not measure only contrast sensitivity. It should also log blind-side detection and reaction time in functional scenes.
Individual variability is large. Field loss alone does not fully predict function. Bowers' driving review states that conventional visual-field extent does not capture compensatory ability and is not enough to predict practical driving performance. This supports repeated personal measurement. Eric's baseline and progress matter more than generic averages.
Scanning quality matters: not scanning, scanning late, or scanning too narrowly can cause missed blind-side hazards. Driving With Hemianopia IV reports impaired blind-side pedestrian detection at intersections related to absent or insufficient head scanning. NegletFix can safely measure large leftward head turns, left-target detection, and reaction time without making driving claims.
Head-scan magnitude can be quantified. Driving With Hemianopia VII found blind-side gaze/head scan thresholds predictive of hazard detection, including about 19-27 degrees of head movement depending on the detection target. Quest head tracking makes this directly measurable in VR: scan angle, scan timing, and detection success can become app metrics.
Auditory reminders may improve proactive blind-side scanning in simulated intersections. Driving With Hemianopia IX found more early large blind-side scans with audio cues and faster responses to hazards in a post hoc simulator analysis. Audio cues are relevant to a VR functional-assessment task, but should remain a training/measurement idea, not a real-road aid.
Peripheral prisms/Peli prisms are a compensation tool, especially for obstacle avoidance while walking. The JAMA Ophthalmology randomized crossover trial found real peripheral prisms more helpful than sham for obstacle avoidance when walking. A prior community-based trial reported continued wear and perceived mobility benefit in a subset. Prisms belong in a clinician/accommodation branch. They do not replace the restorative AV training protocol.
Driving rules vary by location and should be handled separately from rehab training. Bowers' review summarizes variable visual-field requirements across jurisdictions. NANOS also warns that driving poses a hazard for many people with HH. NegletFix language should say "functional vision tracking," never "driving readiness."

4. What Changes For The Project

Promote: Functional Metrics

Track left-side detection rate, reaction time, scan angle, and whether the scan happened before the target/hazard became urgent.

Promote: Transfer Questions

Ask whether clinical gains transfer to walking, screen use, room navigation, crowded places, and confidence outside the house.

Park: Driving Clearance

The app must not produce any driving score. That would be clinically and legally unsafe.

Park: Prisms As Protocol Core

Peli/prism glasses are interesting for low-vision referral and accommodation, but they are not the AV restoration mechanism we are building.

Design translation
Sprint 2 suggests a future Quest task: a low-stress "left-side mobility scan" scene where Eric keeps moving through a virtual corridor or street-like environment, detects left-side events, and the app logs scan angle, scan timing, detection, and reaction time. This would be a measurement/training task, not a road-driving simulator.

5. Suggested App Metrics

Metric Why It Matters How To Measure In Quest
Left blind-side detection rate Directly reflects whether targets/hazards are noticed on the affected side. Spawn targets or motion events at fixed left eccentricities; log hit/miss.
Reaction time to left targets Function can improve even if field size barely changes; faster detection may be meaningful. Timestamp target onset and controller response; compare left vs right and session-to-session.
Peak leftward head scan angle Bowers' driving work shows scan magnitude matters. Quest tracking can capture this reliably. Log headset yaw from fixation/forward reference during each trial window.
Early proactive scan rate Late scanning may detect too late for real function. Early scans are the useful habit. Score whether a scan above threshold occurs before the target appears or before a warning deadline.
Navigation confidence / collision notes Eric's lived function matters: crowded places, obstacle avoidance, left-side surprise events. Short post-session questionnaire; optional notes after real-world walks.

6. Watchlist

Good next research branch
Peripheral prisms deserve a small, separate clinician/accommodation note. They may be useful to ask a neuro-ophthalmologist or low-vision specialist about, but they should not be mixed into the AV training outcome claims.
Still unknown
Whether a VR scanning task will transfer to real walking and crowded environments is a research question. The safe way to handle it is to measure both VR performance and Eric's daily-function notes, then compare over time.
