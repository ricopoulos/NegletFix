Medication + Neuroplasticity Doctor Brief

A cautious review of prescription or supplement-like candidates that might come up when asking whether medication can boost response to visual rehabilitation after stroke.

Checked 2026-05-26 Eric: chronic right PCA stroke, July 2021 Target: left HH + contrast/dimness Use: doctor conversation only

1. Bottom Line

Clinical boundary
I found no medication with proven efficacy for chronic homonymous hemianopia recovery or for boosting NegletFix-style visual rehabilitation in Eric's exact condition. Any medication discussion belongs with the neurologist, neuro-ophthalmologist, or prescribing physician.

The honest conclusion is not "never talk about medication." It is: do not ask for a generic neuroplasticity enhancer. Ask whether any medication is clinically justified to remove barriers to training, such as depression, anxiety, sleep disruption, fatigue, medication side effects, or a separately diagnosed attentional neglect syndrome.

2. What Is Most Worth Asking The Doctor

  1. Medication cleanup first: can the doctor review current prescriptions and supplements for sedation, anticholinergic burden, dizziness, hyponatremia risk, sleep disruption, blood-pressure effects, or fall risk that could reduce training tolerance?
  2. Fatigue as a real treatment target: if neuro-fatigue is blocking consistent sessions, is there a safe post-stroke fatigue treatment strategy? Modafinil has a small trial signal for fatigue, not vision restoration.
  3. Fluoxetine: is there any reason to consider an SSRI for Eric's visual recovery, given that the direct FLUORESCE signal was acute-only and the larger general-stroke fluoxetine evidence does not support routine recovery use?
  4. Levodopa: does the doctor consider dopaminergic adjunct therapy inappropriate after DARS and ESTREL, or is there any monitored research-like scenario after a clean no-drug training baseline?
  5. Neglect vs hemianopia: does Eric have true spatial neglect in addition to left homonymous hemianopia? If not, neglect-targeted drugs like methylphenidate or rotigotine should stay parked.

3. Candidate Evidence Matrix

Candidate Status What The Evidence Says Relevance To Eric Conversation Stance
Fluoxetine Parked FLUORESCE randomized 17 acute ischemic-stroke patients with isolated HH to fluoxetine 20 mg/day vs placebo within 10 days. The completed group was tiny, and the primary endpoint showed a nonsignificant trend. A 2024 individual-patient meta-analysis of 5907 recent-stroke patients found no functional recovery benefit and higher seizure, fall-injury, fracture, and hyponatremia risk. Directly visual-field related, but acute-only. Eric is chronic since 2021, so transfer is weak. Ask only as a literature item. Use clinically for depression/anxiety only if indicated, not as a visual-recovery protocol.
Levodopa / co-careldopa Weak Old small motor-stroke trial was positive, but DARS 2019 and ESTREL 2025 did not show meaningful motor-recovery benefit when levodopa was added to rehabilitation. No direct HH evidence. Newer motor-rehab evidence weakens the old rationale. Low-priority doctor question. Consider only after a clean no-adjunct training block, and only if the physician sees a safe rationale.
Modafinil Symptom target MIDAS was a small randomized crossover trial in post-stroke fatigue showing reduced fatigue and improved quality of life during 6 weeks of 200 mg/day therapy. Not a visual-recovery drug. Could matter if fatigue prevents training repetition. Ask as fatigue management, not neuroplasticity enhancement. Physician must screen blood pressure, sleep, anxiety, seizure risk, and interactions.
Methylphenidate Park Some neglect/prism-adaptation literature exists, but AHA/ASA stroke-rehab guidance says dextroamphetamine or methylphenidate is not recommended to facilitate motor recovery. Only relevant if a clinician confirms attention/arousal impairment or true spatial neglect. HH alone is not the same target. Do not frame as visual-field medicine. Ask only if the doctor identifies a separate attention syndrome.
Rotigotine Neglect only A small double-blind RCT in 16 right-hemisphere stroke patients with hemispatial neglect improved visual search/cancellation while on rotigotine. Interesting because it touches left-sided visual search, but it is neglect/attention, not occipital HH restoration. Ask only after diagnostic clarification: HH, neglect, or both?
D-amphetamine / amphetamine Avoid Historic rehabilitation-adjunct literature has weak replication and safety concerns; not a routine stroke-recovery medication. Not appropriate for Eric's self-directed visual rehab plan. Off the table outside a formal trial or a very specific specialist indication.
Donepezil Low Exploratory stroke studies exist for cognition or as motor-therapy adjunct, but no HH visual-restoration evidence was found. Could matter only if formal neuropsychology finds a treatable cognitive issue affecting training. Ask only as cognition/attention care, not visual-field recovery.
Citicoline Low The large ICTUS acute ischemic-stroke trial was stopped for futility and found no efficacy for moderate-to-severe acute ischemic stroke. Cochrane evidence is not strong enough for clear recovery benefit. No chronic HH evidence. Low-stakes supplement-style discussion only if doctor approves safety and interactions.
Cerebrolysin / peptides / biohacking compounds Avoid Broader neurorecovery reviews include many registered pharmacological classes, but only limited high-certainty routine evidence exists, and these compounds are not standard visual-rehab care. Very low; large distraction risk. Do not pursue outside regulated care or a formal trial.

4. Practical Doctor Script

Suggested wording
"I am doing a structured visual rehabilitation project for chronic left homonymous hemianopia after a right PCA stroke. I am not asking for a magic neuroplasticity drug. I want to know whether any medication has a safe, evidence-based role to help me tolerate and repeat the training, or whether we should avoid adding a drug because the evidence for visual recovery is too weak."

Bring These Details

  • Stroke type and date: right PCA stroke, July 2021, chronic phase.
  • Current visual target: left homonymous hemianopia with severe left contrast deficit and persistent dimness/gray overlay.
  • Objective baseline: left hemifield 0.00 LogCS, right hemifield 2.25 LogCS, central about 1.05+ LogCS.
  • Training plan: structured audiovisual/visual rehab, measured before and after; no drug should be added casually because it confounds attribution.
  • Current medications, supplements, blood pressure history, seizure history, falls/fractures, sodium history, glaucoma/eye pressure history, sleep/fatigue pattern, depression/anxiety symptoms.

5. Suggested NegletFix Sequence

  1. First block: run the visual/audiovisual training without any new medication adjunct.
  2. Measure: compare against baseline with the same contrast sensitivity and functional tracker tools.
  3. If plateau: bring this brief and the app data to the doctor. Discuss whether fatigue, sleep, depression/anxiety, or medication side effects are limiting training.
  4. If a medication is tried: change only one variable, define a start date, dose, monitoring plan, stop criteria, and pre/post visual measures.
Why sequence matters
If a drug and a new training protocol start at the same time, any improvement becomes hard to interpret. A clean no-adjunct training block protects the science of the project and avoids unnecessary medication risk.
