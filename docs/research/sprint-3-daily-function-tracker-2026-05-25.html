Sprint 3 conclusion
The tracker should sit beside the hard clinical measures. Contrast sensitivity and VR reaction time tell us what changed in the task. The daily-function tracker tells us whether Eric's real life became less visually expensive.
Boundary
This is not a diagnostic score and not proof of visual-field restoration. It is a structured symptom and function log to compare against session data, baseline contrast sensitivity, and future clinician feedback.
Evidence caution
Visual scanning and reading work are legitimate rehab themes, but the evidence is mixed. Eye-Search reported improved impaired-field search speed, while the larger SEARCH trial found both visual scanning and sham groups improved without a significant between-group difference. That is exactly why this tracker logs daily function instead of declaring a treatment effect.

3. Verified Daily Domains

Tracker Domain Why It Belongs Verification Anchor
Glare / dimness / brightness tolerance Eric's own target includes persistent bilateral dimness. Episode 274 adds bright-day, overcast, and visual-overload triggers. Cleveland Clinic notes dimmed or dark visual experience can occur with HH. RNIB lists light sensitivity among stroke-related visual symptoms.
Screen tolerance Both episodes point to computer/screen load: screen aggravation in 274, large-font/cursor/TTS accommodations in 156. American Stroke Association notes post-stroke visual difficulties can include reading problems and visual processing issues. Screen use should be logged as a near-vision load.
Reading effort Episode 156 is especially strong here: large print, rereading, word-by-word effort, and fatigue after only a few emails. RNIB, NICE/NCBI, and Cleveland Clinic all connect hemianopia to reading difficulty and compensatory strategies.
Visual search / clutter / figure-ground Episode 156 mentions figure-ground, visual perception, search tasks, word searches, and simple mazes. RNIB explicitly recommends scanning, puzzles, word search games, and computer-based scanning programs to improve visual perception and tracking skills.
Mobility / scanning confidence Sprint 2 already made mobility measurable. Sprint 3 adds crowded environments and car-travel load from lived experience. RNIB notes day-to-day issues such as locating things, traffic, and crowded environments. Sprint 2 covers Bowers' hazard-detection evidence.
Neurological fatigue / recovery next day Episode 274 flags overdoing and stimulation-heavy days. Episode 156 describes neurological fatigue from reading and email. Fatigue is common after stroke, but this tracker keeps it specific: visual fatigue after visual load, training, screens, reading, or crowds.

4. The Tracker

Use one row per AV training session. The scoring is deliberately simple: higher number means harder day.

0-4 Difficulty Scale

0 - none
No meaningful difficulty today.
1 - mild
Noticeable, but did not change the plan for the day.
2 - moderate
Needed an adaptation: sunglasses, hat, larger text, rest break, text-to-speech, reduced screen time, or quieter environment.
3 - strong
Had to stop, avoid, leave, or substantially shorten the activity.
4 - severe
Function was blocked, symptoms were unsafe, or the effect carried into the next day.
Column Record Why
glare_dimness_0_4 How hard brightness, glare, dimness, or gray overlay felt today. Eric's most important lived symptom beyond the field cut.
screen_tolerance_0_4 Difficulty using computer/phone/tablet after training. Captures whether daily digital work becomes easier or more costly.
reading_effort_0_4 Difficulty reading text, email, subtitles, or documents. Reading may improve through compensation even if visual field size does not change.
visual_search_clutter_0_4 Difficulty finding objects in clutter, menus, shelves, desktop, or busy environments. Maps to figure-ground/search problems from episode 156 and scanning evidence.
mobility_scanning_confidence_0_4 Difficulty moving through rooms, streets, stores, or crowded places. Connects Sprint 2 mobility risk to lived daily confidence.
neuro_fatigue_0_4 Visual/brain fatigue after training or daily visual load. Captures the cost of seeing, not just whether the task was completed.
next_morning_recovery_0_4 Whether symptoms settled by the next morning. Separates productive training load from prolonged regression.

5. Weekly Review

Every 5 Sessions

  • Average each 0-4 domain for the last 5 completed sessions.
  • Mark best day and worst day.
  • List the top two triggers: bright light, screen, reading, clutter, crowd, car travel, poor sleep, or training load.
  • Compare with VR data: left detection, reaction time, head-scan behavior, and contrast checks when available.

What Counts As Useful

  • Lower daily difficulty at the same real-life workload.
  • More screen or reading minutes before fatigue.
  • Faster next-morning recovery after visual load.
  • More confidence in cluttered or crowded places.
  • Objective VR improvement plus daily-function improvement in the same direction.

6. Safety Rules

Stop and escalate
New sudden vision loss, new neurological symptoms, severe headache, new confusion, new weakness, or symptoms that feel stroke-like are emergency issues, not tracker entries. Cleveland Clinic explicitly treats new HH as urgent because it can signal stroke or brain bleed.
Training load rule
If a session produces a 4 in headache/eye pain, neuro fatigue, or next-morning recovery, reduce the next session load and flag it for review before continuing the full protocol.
