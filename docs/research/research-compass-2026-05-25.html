Working framing
The research question is not "can VR help stroke?" in general. The useful question is narrower: can repeated, measured, audiovisual or visual-perceptual training improve detection, reaction time, contrast sensitivity, scanning, or daily function in chronic post-stroke homonymous hemianopia, especially near the left-field scotoma border?

2. Research Tracks

Each track should have its own queue, confidence level, and "so what?" decision for NegletFix. This prevents generic neuroscience content from flooding the project.

Core: same condition, same problem

Highest priority. These sources directly affect protocol design, expected outcomes, and measurement.

  • Post-stroke homonymous hemianopia
  • Visual field defects after PCA / occipital stroke
  • Contrast sensitivity after V1 damage
  • Scotoma-border training
  • Reaction time and functional transfer

Near: useful mechanisms and delivery

Useful when it informs the training design, but it needs translation back to Eric's case.

  • Audiovisual / multisensory rehabilitation
  • VR visual rehabilitation
  • Visual perceptual learning
  • Blindsight, superior colliculus, pulvinar, extrastriate routes
  • Home telerehabilitation and adherence

Adjunct: promising but not the engine

Track carefully, but do not let these replace the evidence-backed audiovisual program.

  • EEG neurofeedback for stroke and attention
  • Consumer EEG validity, especially Muse TP9 / TP10
  • tDCS / tACS paired with visual training
  • BCI plus rehabilitation task designs
  • Medication adjuncts for clinician discussion only

Watchlist: lead sources, not evidence

Good for discovering papers, researchers, talks, or trials. Weak as primary evidence.

  • YouTube lectures and long interviews
  • X / Twitter posts by labs and clinicians
  • Conference talks and posters
  • Patient stories and rehab-device demos
  • Company claims that need independent verification

3. Questions Worth Hunting

Clinical and measurement questions

  • What outcomes improve most reliably in chronic hemianopia: contrast sensitivity, reaction time, reading speed, navigation, scanning, or quality of life?
  • How stable is contrast sensitivity over time without training?
  • Which changes exceed test-retest noise and matter in daily life?
  • Does the "gray overlay" map to contrast sensitivity, luminance perception, fatigue, attention, or another visual-processing variable?

Protocol questions

  • Which dose is realistic for chronic stroke: 5 hours, 15 hours, 60 sessions, or more?
  • Should stimuli start near the scotoma border before expanding outward?
  • Do audiovisual pairs need strict synchronization, or is a looser window sufficient?
  • What should we measure every 5 sessions so progress is visible without exhausting Eric?

Technology questions

  • Is Quest 2 good enough for the first 30-session program?
  • Does Quest 3 materially improve contrast, comfort, tracking, or adherence?
  • Can Muse detect a reliable eyes-open vs eyes-closed alpha effect for Eric?
  • If Muse signal quality is weak, should EEG remain informational rather than gating the reward?

Discovery questions

  • Which labs are publishing most actively on hemianopia rehabilitation now?
  • Are there active or upcoming trials that match chronic post-stroke visual field defect?
  • Which YouTube channels or podcasts repeatedly discuss neuroplasticity, visual rehab, BCI, or neuro-ophthalmology?
  • Can long videos reveal useful names, papers, or hypotheses that standard search misses?

4. Source Pipeline

This is the practical workflow for turning papers, trials, videos, and social posts into organized research instead of scattered bookmarks.

Capture

Save the source with URL, title, author / channel, date, source type, and why it looked relevant. Do not summarize yet.

Triage

Assign it to one track: core, near, adjunct, or watchlist. If it is generic brain-health content with no clear connection to hemianopia or stroke visual rehab, park it.

Extract

Pull only useful claims, timestamps, terms, names, cited papers, protocols, outcome measures, or trial IDs. Long YouTube videos should become timestamped notes, not full prose summaries.

Verify

Check medical claims against PubMed, DOI pages, ClinicalTrials.gov, guidelines, or the original paper. X and YouTube can trigger a lead; they should not settle a claim.

Organize

Promote verified findings to the wiki if they change project knowledge. Use HTML pages for readable synthesis, diagrams, and decision maps.

Decide

Every finding should end with one of four decisions: affects protocol, affects measurement, affects hardware, or interesting but no action yet.

5. YouTube and Transcript Mining

Good use case
A 9-hour neuroscience video is not a thing to watch linearly. It is a searchable transcript. The goal is to extract timestamps that mention relevant ideas, then use those moments as research leads.

What to extract

  • Timestamps where visual field loss, stroke, blindsight, contrast sensitivity, superior colliculus, EEG, neurofeedback, tDCS, tACS, VR rehab, or visual perceptual learning are discussed.
  • Names of researchers, labs, studies, papers, devices, or clinical trials.
  • Claims that are specific enough to verify.
  • Potential new search terms.

What not to overvalue

  • Generic neuroplasticity claims without a paper or population.
  • Motivational recovery stories that do not match chronic occipital stroke.
  • Neurofeedback claims without sham-control evidence.
  • Commercial device claims without independent validation.
Input Process Output
YouTube URL with captions Fetch transcript, chunk by timestamp, search using NegletFix terms. Timestamped digest with candidate leads.
YouTube URL without captions Extract audio, transcribe, then run the same timestamp search. Transcript plus extracted topic moments.
Interesting timestamp Summarize locally, then verify claims through medical sources. Research note or wiki candidate.
Channel with many videos Build a queue: title, date, duration, transcript availability, relevance score. Watchlist ranked by likely relevance.

9. Guardrails

Medical caution
This file is a research organization tool, not medical advice. Protocol, device, medication, stimulation, and clinical decisions should stay grounded in verified evidence and clinician input where appropriate.
  • Do not let generic neurofeedback content outrank direct hemianopia evidence.
  • Do not treat YouTube, X, or company material as proof.
  • Keep the current Unity path simple: open-loop audiovisual training first, EEG as optional adjunct after signal validation.
  • Separate measured results from hope. The baseline and future contrast sensitivity logs are the anchor.
